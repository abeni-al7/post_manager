Fire Brigade Fails to Extinguish Flames

  
 

 

  
     
 
 















 

  

During the ceremony when Mayor Berhane Deressa was handed the key to the capital on May 9, 2006, he made a wistful statement, since proven just a dream, "The new cabinet is a fire brigade that will put out the fires of political tension in Addis Abeba."
 

But the Addis Ababa City Caretaker Administration, whose official time comes to a close next month, has performed far from the fire fighter style saviour that the Mayor had hoped.

 

Beyene Gebre, resident of Kirkos District's former Kebele 25, and avid follower of city level politics, has been paying attention to the Caretaker Administration over the past year, and none of the promises that he had heard with hope have become realities.
 

"They made a lot of noise about political reconciliation between the EPRDF and CUD, but they did not even bother to speak to us, the residents who were most affected by the whole situation," said Beyene.

 

It was because of the May 2005 election violence that Prime Minister Meles Zenawi appointed the temporary Caretaker Administration for a year, until fresh elections could be held.

 

Failure to advance in the political route is the least of the downfalls of the Administration, according to an anonymous investor. Granted an investment license from the city's Investment Authority in September 2006, he had plans to begin a plastic manufacturing plant. He requested a 4,000sqm plot from the city's Investment Authority, but has yet to receive a response.
 

"I have just about had it," he said "I am seriously considering forgetting about the whole thing."
 

The City Administration has industrial zones where it gives plots for manufacturing industries. But, because of a lack of adequate infrastructure, there has not been a single industry zone that has began operations since the Caretaker came into power.
 

Aside from the Akaki Kaliti and Mekanisa Lebu Industrial Zones, established by the Provisional City Administration under Arkebe Okubay, there are an additional four zones with a total area of 687ht that have yet to be put to use.
 

The Akaki Kaliti Industrial Zone has requested an additional 62ht for an expansion project, which has yet to see the light of day. The Jimma Road Industrial Zone, 282ht, freight terminal, 35ht, and the Kilinto Industrial Zone, 305ht, have all been sitting idle.
 

With a year long vision to decrease joblessness, housing shortages, and increase infrastructure, among 10 other goals, the Administration has not been able to meet its goals. 5.16 billion Br budget was allocated for the 2006/2007 fiscal year, with 3.6 billion Br allocated to capital projects. Of this amount the 152.6 million Br that was assigned to land development, 312.2 million for potable water projects, 1.1 billion Br for road constriction, and 1.7 billion Br for housing development, have yet to be put to good use. The rest of the money is allocated to health, 50.5 million Br, and education, 17.9 million Br.
 

Although it is faced with over 1,000 pending plot requests there has not been a single investor that was awarded a plot in an industrial zone until now.

 

Land development and potable water projects have been especially thorny issues for the Caretaker Administration and the huge condominium housing projects have been no better.
 

Realising the enormity of the problem, a committee comprised of members from the Trade and Industry Development Bureau, Land Development and Administration Authority, Infrastructure and Construction Permit Authority, the Investment Authority and the Addis Abeba City Roads Authority (AACRA), headed by Wubshet Berhanu (PhD), general manager of the Caretaker Administration, was formed three months ago.
 

Yet the expectation that the committee would use the allocated budget to complete the necessary infrastructure in the industrial zones was yet another dashed hope failing to materialise.

 

A member of the committee told Fortune that so far it has only been meeting.
 

"No action has been taken, no decisions have been made, nothing is getting done," he said. 

 

But nothing being done has not only affected the investors who have been patiently waiting, but the Administration as well. With plans to collect 5.16 billion Br in revenues, 1.2 billion was supposed to be earned from leasing out plots.
 

With the fiscal year ending in June 2007, meeting the billion Birr mark does not seem plausible in a mere two months time.
 

"With the Lease Board unable to make speedy decisions to plot requests, how can this money be collected?" said an official at the Administration.
 

Fortune's source, close to the Board, agrees with this assessment.
 

"When you compare it to the previous Lease Board under the Provisional Administration there is a huge gap in productivity," said the source. "If the former Board had 60 requests to assess, then the meeting would end with 60 decisions."

 

Not so, though, with the existing Lease Board, which has problems passing speedy and efficient decisions, according to him.
 

"Maybe, on a good day, five agendas are looked at and less than that will get decisions" he added.

 

Whether through indecisiveness or lack of infrastructure, investors have not been able to develop plots under the existing Administration. Part of the infrastructure that has not been put in place at industrial zones is water lines.
 

Water shortages, which have increased from 30pc of Addis Abeba's population to 48pc, according to AAWSA's findings, have been crippling blow to getting the Administration's visions realised.
 

Although Dire Dam's flow is being purified at Legedadi and entering the main system since 1998, the project, considered crucial to curbing the water shortage of the capital, has seen nothing but glitches.
 

With plans to dig 30 wells to provide urgently needed potable water to the city during the current budget year, delay after delay has made this another vision that the Administration can stack under unaccomplished. With just two short months until the end of the budget year, hope is not glimmering here.
 

This problem, its depth and magnitude, has led to the replacement of the General Manager of the Addis Abeba Water and Sewage Authority (AAWSA), Seyoum Desta, and his Deputy General Manager, Zewdu Teffera, from their positions.
 

The Administration, with its well project, had intended to increase the 74.4 million cubic metre water supplies to the capital to 86.4 million cubic metres, which would have aided it in a plan to even out water distribution.
 

But water, land and politics are not the only items on the test. Perhaps a most daring shortcoming of the Caretaker Administration is its extreme delays in condominium housing projects. Having set plans to begin construction on 33,000 homes and complete 32,000 others from the last budget year, it had allocated a 1.7 billon Br budget for this purpose. It had even put aside 856 million Br from its total budget to cover compensation costs.
 

The Administration has only begun construction on 5,000 units, and even this was begun only a month ago in the Gofa Camp area in the Nifas Silk Lafto District.
 

"This is all because of the drastic increase in contracting costs," said the official.  "And then of course there is the fact that the Administration has not been following a strict timetable."

Addis Abeba is just the beginning of a large scale, four year national housing campaign spearheaded by the Ministry of Works and Urban Development (MoWUD). The Ministry has allocated 24 billion Br, obtained on loan from the Commercial Bank of Ethiopia (CBE), to build 400,000 homes in 72 towns across the country.
 

The Ministry of Finance and Economic Development (MoFED) guaranteed the loan for the project on behalf of the regional governments.
 

But the Caretaker Administration does not need to find financing or trudge deep in debt, with the funds already in its account, and yet, no usage. Unsatisfied with the city's working process and the endless delays, MoWAD has begun preparations to undertake an overhaul in the working systems of 10 districts and the Addis Abeba City Housing Development Office, according to the source.
 

"With the lack of housing and employment opportunities, sanitation is just another headache," said a resident of Bole District. "Since the former Administration stepped down litter, beggars are back in full force and plastic shanty towns are everywhere."
 

A source in the Caretaker Cabinet that asked to remain anonymous because of the sensitivity of the issue told Fortune that the Administration, led by Berhane, would have another year to make improvements.
 

"Prime Minister Meles Zenawi called the Cabinet to a meeting one month ago and informed us that the election would not likely be happening with the upcoming Millennium," said the Cabinet member. "Since we are going to be around for the 2007/2008 budget year, we have already started forecasts and preparations."
 

But Beyene is more distressed at the idea of the Caretaker Administration continuing its tenure than any other immediate issue.
 

"If they continue in office, what should we expect for the next year, more of what we have already seen?" he lamented.
 

"The city is dirtier and shows no signs of improving; infrastructure construction is causing outages in water, telephone and electric services to various offices, while my neighbourhood is full of jobless youths whose numbers, overall, are increasing. Everywhere that you turn, you see people affected by inflation, unable to meet their basic needs because everything is so expensive. Addis Abeba is tearing at the seams, and we get stuck with another year of inactivity from our town hall? The disappointing irony here is that all 10 members of the Cabinet are educated and have reasonable amounts of work experience. They are able to solve these problems, but they have not solved them yet," fumed Beyene.

 

By WUDINEH ZENEBE

FORTUNE STAFF WRITER
