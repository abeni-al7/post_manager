Robin Hood Did His Homework
     








 

The recent surtax introduced by the Ethiopian government on April 11, 2007 seems at first to be well intentioned and accurately targeted. The tax on so-called luxury items is being marketed as a way for the government to pay for wheat subsidies for urban dwellers suffering under the brunt of high inflation, reaching an estimated 13.6pc. This Robin Hood policy of taxing the rich to feed the poor would be a cleverly conceived measure to tackle pressing problems if it meets some important criteria, by no means clearly addressed at the onset of the policy.


The tax must be efficiently and cost effectively enforced with low administrative costs relative to revenue, targeted at specific consumer luxury items so as not to harm business investment or already stressed poor citizens, levied upon items with low elasticity (responsiveness in demand to the increase in price), and supplemented with a campaign to make the public and merchants aware of the specific nature of the tax so that it does not add to general inflation. Although not available to the public, a comprehensive study on these issues should have been completed before implementing the surtax, answering questions such as why 10pc and not a different fraction, why the specific items were selected, what the projected revenue and costs are, and the implementation plans and subsequent progress reports.
 

Surtax, in its most basic form, is an additional tax on items already subject to some form of excise. In the current case, 10pc surtax will be levied on imported goods, such as automobiles, clothes, packed food, electronics, beverages, jewellery and perfumes. In order for the tax to raise the revenue projected by the government, it must be sure that the increase in the price does not cause so many consumers to stop buying the taxed goods that revenue actually falls, as the effect of a higher income per product is overwhelmed by the vast decrease in the number of products from which revenue is obtained.
 

Similarly, it is a delicate issue to ensure that all of the goods taxed are being used for personal consumption and not business investment. In a country with so many unregistered businesses and a thriving informal market, it is a fine issue to asses whether the use of automobiles and electronics especially, are for consumption alone. If it is the case that some of the taxed items' end use is for business purposes, the result is higher prices for consumers (inflation) and a drop in the growth rate for economic activity from levels it otherwise would have achieved.

In order for this undesired scenario to be avoided, after the fact of detailed preliminary cost-benefit analysis, strict enforcement and low administrative costs are crucial. By raising the price of imported goods, the incentive for already acknowledged prevalent smuggling activities is increased. With higher profit potential, the smugglers are more likely to engage in the illegal business, especially without an increased level of border enforcement. However, the plan for a corresponding programme to curb illegal trafficking was glaringly absent in the previous week's announcement. The costs of such enforcement must not outweigh the additional revenue of the tax; otherwise the whole effort is for naught.
 

Other administrative costs added to the already stressed government budget will be increased, including customs personnel with subsequent training, data keeping and checking, compliance inspections and the like. Where is the comprehensive report to detail all the expenditures associated with collecting this projected revenue?
 

The government has a responsibility to undertake studies and distribute information of this nature such that it may be assessed by outside sources and so that the public may be confident of the legitimacy of promises for help. If these measures are taken it gives the public and opposition groups the opportunity to construct valid second opinions and hold the government accountable to the policies they create; a situation far from current realities.
 

The purported reason for implementing this surtax is to add revenue to the government's budget, currently facing a close to 80pc of Gross Domestic Product (GDP) deficit, in order to subsidise wheat, a substitute grain for teff, for the urban poor. According to Prime Minister Meles Zenawi, food prices in general increased 14.4pc while grain increased 16.6pc in the last year. In this scenario, the 150,000 to 200,000qt of wheat to be obtained from foreign grants, along with government purchases, to provide the urban poor with around 25Kg of wheat each, every four to six weeks, seems like a policy with great insight. The poor are disproportionately harmed by rising basic goods costs as they spend a higher portion of their income on the items, such as wheat, while the revenue to pay for the grain is a tax on luxury items whose consumption is skewed towards the wealthy. In a country with a Gini Coefficient of around 0.6 (a measure of income inequality), quite high by any standards, it is clear that equitable growth means a transfer of wealth from the rich to the poor.
 

But where is the analysis to show that the tax will raise the revenue to pay for this subsidy? If the fiscal deficit is increased, the result will be more inflationary pressure. Similarly, the administrative costs of the grain programme and the detriments to the millions of farmers who depend on teff income and will be facing increased competition from the wheat substitute, and thus lower prices, must be carefully examined to determine the efficacy of the surtax.
 

Although the government has shrewdly announced the tax in tandem with the grain programme, making an association of higher consumer prices with helping the poor that few could argue with, the intricacies of a government budget are not so simple. It is not as if every Birr gained from this specific source of revenue, the surtax, is being spent directly on grain imports; rather a government's revenue is fungible.
 

While the grain subsidy is pricey, it is likely that the costs of the Somali military campaign, although unknown to the public, exceed the wheat expenditures and thus, must be a more pressing budgetary concern. It is also to be remembered that the last surtax levied in Ethiopia, Proclamation 59/1999, was sold as a method to pay for the 1999/2000 Eritrea conflict. The surtax is a more difficult sell if funding a war costing many Ethiopian lives is the rationale, however.
 

In levying a tax on a highly uneducated public, the government has a responsibility to coherently state the rationale, extent and enforcement procedure of the tax. This is not to say that every citizen should understand the complexities of the economic theory concerning taxes; but if the anecdotal reports of higher prices on untaxed basic goods like eggs (not experiencing the usual decline following the Easter Holiday) are any indication, this tax is leading to inflation and is hurting the poor. The economic theory to support an increase in the price of eggs because a tax has been placed on perfume is extremely tenuous; however, the consumer must be informed enough to argue back to the merchant claiming a new tax has increased his supply costs and thus that he is justified in charging a higher price.
 

A campaign for the information to reach the masses in a straightforward manner is to be expected. At the least, a couple radio or ETV advertisements may be in order.

 

Overall, the new surtax leaves a lot to be questioned. To the undiscerning ear, the idea to tax the rich to feed the poor is flawless. However, upon a deeper examination it is unclear if the necessary precautions have been undertaken to ensure that the tax achieves its objectives. It is the responsibility of the government to take greater care and effort in implementing these far reaching policies.
