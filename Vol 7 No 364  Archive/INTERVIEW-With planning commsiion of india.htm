Dr Kirit S Parikh is a member of the Planning Commission for the government of India with the rank of a Minister of State. He was in Addis Abeba last week for the conference on Development Planning that was held by African Ministers of Finance at the Economic Commission for Africa. Fortune Staff Writer LULIT AMDEMARIAM spoke to Dr Parikh on Millennium Development Goals and the lessons that a developing country such as Ethiopia can take from India and what the Asian country can do together with African economies to strengthen their move towards globalisation and a market system.

 

Ethiopia, India: Worlds Apart, Common Issues
     
 
 


 
 

Fortune: What are some of the lessons that Ethiopia can learn from India?

 

Dr Parikh: The problems that can clearly be identified by simply looking around, those of power and infrastructure are some of the same issues that India is confronted with today. Yes there is progress on the part of both countries, but the concerns are shared ones.
 

India also worries about increasing agricultural production and getting potable water to the entire population. The same investments and advances are being made in electrification but power outages are something that is still experienced. There are a number of things that one can learn. Just to give a brief background, the Indian economy grew from the 1950s to the 1970s by about 3.5pc every year. In the 1980s and 1990s the rate increased to five per cent for this and the next decade; the growth rate is expected to increase to between seven and eight per cent.
 

What we found helped achieve this steady increase was our emphasis on education. We developed a strong high school and college level system that was able to produce a high calibre work force that the market was able to handle at the time.
 

But with this progress not enough attention was paid to primary and elementary education. This is why we still have a high rate of illiteracy. But that is a major lesson that can be learnt.
 

Education development should be inclusive of the entire population, and so in our 11th five year plan that came into effect on April 1, 2007, we have set a target of reaching more sectors of the society in terms of education. This will hopefully lead to a faster and more inclusive growth.
 

In addition, we are focusing on other social targets that would ensure every child goes to school, every habitation has clean drinking water and every village is electrified and connected with a road.
 

There are also other issues like the guarantee that is provided by the government to provide a minimum 100 paid working days a year to every citizen at minimum wage. If the work is not available, then they have the right to claim the wages in compensation.
 

The whole idea of this is that we want to bridge the divides between regions, communities, classes and genders. We have a set of very well defined targets that have projected end dates, but there are often cases when the time limit is not met by the goals and accusations of incompletion are thrown around.
 

But the solution here is to monitor the targets that have been set. If you want to improve primary education, how can you guarantee that the students will attend every day with the lack of infrastructure. Perhaps you can offer a warm lunch meal for free as an incentive.
 

What about teacher absenteeism? An incentive can be taken here as well. But even then, how is it that you can guarantee that the students are receiving the education that they are supposed to? Here a possible solution is to install a standardised testing system for various age groups. So if close monitoring is done on targets, then achieving them is not a difficult feat.
 

Then there is the issue of transparency and accountability. For instance, if the water company in our country claims that they have provided drinking water to a village, then that village must be placed on their Website. Those very same villagers are also entitled to find out how much money has been allocated to their region and how much project implementation costs are. Transparency in the use of funds and the implementation of projects will improve the long term outcomes and lead to a better mechanism.
 

You mentioned that higher education was the key factor in the development of India. Ethiopia has been making significant changes to the existing educational system. What experiences can India’s development lend to Ethiopia in this regard?

 

India invested lots of money in top class institutions. When in the 1950s it was realised that the country must have technical institutions on the calibre of the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT). Now this has produced some of the best and the brightest of corporate engineers that have been vital to the international growth of IT. For a long time, there was no use for the people that the higher education institutions were producing. About 90pc immigrated to the US and Europe and played huge parts in the development of Silicon Valley and IT.
 

This instilled in India a sense of confidence and ability which was seen through the development of local companies that were able to absorb the educated manpower that the country was producing. I was told that the 90pc figure had dropped to an astounding seven per cent. Now those very same people are able to get those very same advantages and job opportunities in the country allowing for increasing growth and improvement in the standard of living.
 

In addition we also developed management institutes in collaboration with Western universities. This is a critical element that has really helped us. India now has a huge skilled manpower force that includes vocationally trained people, this has increased domestic demand. Ethiopia should take heed from this and not neglect higher education.
 

India can be a good partner because of the India-Africa E-network programme which will set up a network for distance learning, tele-medicine and tele-education.  A large number of top class Indian institutions are being linked from the other side, but more importantly, once the network has been laid, you will really be able to expand the skill base of the population.
 

The other point to be made here is the integration of markets that has been going on across the world. Technology, which is fast becoming the dominating force for scientists and technologists, is so rapid in its innovations that product life cycles are shorter and shorter, with innovations becoming obsolete in shorter periods of time.
 

Product prices are reduced which also means that those who are producing innovative items need to recover their money as quickly as possible. They want as large a market as possible. Therefore, firms are becoming more and more global. But there is a silver lining to this cloud which is that in the globalised competitive world anyone that is producing any machine breaks up the entire process into different components and out sources the manufacturing to companies all over the world.
 

A single machine can be made up from 300 different parts coming from 30 different countries. A computer may have the same kind of system. So smaller countries that are trying to join the race should take advantage of this outsourcing; look at the boom that was experienced in India when call centres from Europe and the United States. This is the same effective strategy that can be implemented by countries like Ethiopia.
 
 

The IMF and Prime Minister Meles Zenawi recently placed local inflation rates at 13pc. What sort of recommendations can be made to decrease the rise in inflation rates?

First, the sources of that inflation must be looked at. If the inflation is caused by excessive spending on the import of different goods but there is no local productivity to match that expenditure, then the issue would be to raise productivity, or to come up with manufacturing options that would decrease the amount of imports into the country.

Another factor that can stimulate inflation is the failure of crops. If agriculture outputs fail, particularly for countries that are largely dependant on earnings from the sector, then inflation will more than likely occur. Government overspending on large scale, long term projects that are not seeing any returns but have to pay salaries for staff, may also be another source of inflation.

One needs to balance the consumption expenditure of the government because inflation is a problem that particularly hurts the poor. The rich have assets which increase in value. But the poor have nothing and no means to purchase at high prices.

The more inflation increases, the more interest rates go up as well, and that trend continues on to other aspects of the economy. When interest rates are high, investment is not an attractive option. There are also subsidies put  stress on the government. Where is the funding coming from, outside loans, at the cost of something else, or is it being done by printing more money?

One way to deal with subsidising and government investment in long term projects would be to go into public-private partnerships; Deliver public services at lower costs and more effectively. For instance, in many parts of the world, road projects and other infrastructural investment is handled by the government which awards it to a contractor for construction. The private enterprise is often not interested in top quality work, because it wants the maintenance project which will be issued the following year, if the road were to be of lower quality. In addition, there are often delays, mostly bureaucratic, that make the process even longer.

But in a partnership, a private company handles the construction and maintenance of the road and is paid by the government for its services. Then the company hired has a vested interest and would make more money by building higher quality products, and the end users would benefit immensely.

The private sector has the ability to provide public services in a cost and time effective manner, but the question is a matter of opportunity. This would enable government expenditure to go down, allowing for decrease in inflation.
 

Rural migration is a huge issue in Ethiopia, with the growth of Addis Abeba estimated at 15pc a year. Should the government do more to discourage rural migration which leads to an increase in urban poverty?

 

Obviously, something should be done about the situation with figures like that. But my recommendation would not be to put constraints or physical controls on the migrating populations but rather to set up an incentive structure that would give the rural population a reason to stay where they are.


Why would people migrate if opportunities are available to them right where they are?
 

Agricultural productivity must be increased, irrigation and modern farming techniques developed, quality seeds provided and farmers subsidised, so that the incentives to stay in rural areas are more attractive than uprooting and coming to urban ones.

 

The government is now preparing to begin market intervention, using wheat in order to stabilise prices, but the more consumed grains is teff locally produced. Will market intervention help to regulate prices?

I believe that if the price goes up because there is a shortage of goods, without a decrease in productivity, then you are faced with a problem of hoarding because the seller is waiting for the better price.  But with price increases hoarding is the first thing that becomes a scapegoat. But this is a more intricate issue of market forces of demand and supply. Usually, the hoarder will not stock the goods if the prices are low because they will not be able to earn as much for their products. But when prices are high, whether or not there is a shortage it makes more sense for hoarding because better profits can be made.
 

If there was some sort of a multi-commodity exchange system that offers a more regulated price, then the issue of hoarding would no longer be a factor. Government estimations of crop productivity are also crucial. If the figures show that there will be a shortage, then government has to intervene by importing grains or using its reserves. But intervention is a short term solution; augmenting the supply and increasing productivity are what really need the focus. Crises management is not the answer.
 

Fertilizer is a major commodity that is being imported into the country, with the annual demand reaching 650,000tn this budget year. What kind of growth can be seen in the agricultural sector with the increase in international fertilizer prices and with the strict involvement and control of the government in the fertilizer sector?

We have been faced with a similar problem in India with the increase in fuel prices. Granted India does produce Urea locally, but the country still has to import fertilizer to cater to the demand rates. With the increase in international oil prices, and the prices of fertilizer as well, the government subsidised local producers because they would have otherwise gone out of business.
 

Subsidies are one option, but an even more viable solution is opening up the market to the private sector. The private sector has the capacity to import distribute and sell the commodity with efficiency because it is working for profit. The more they sell and the faster they do it, the more that they will be able to profit. In addition the competition would also make the price affordable for the farmer.
 

On a more general note, do you think that countries that are part of the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) will be able to meet their set visions at the said time?

I am not such an expert on all the countries to have an answer to that question, but I think for many countries at least an effort is being made to attack crucial infrastructural, social, and financial issues. Even if they do not achieve all the goals on the set day, their participation alone would certainly achieve substantial growth and changes in their respective countries. To be honest, India may not be able to achieve the MDGs but we will achieve perhaps many other goals which are socio-economic and not part of the MDG plan. What is important is that action is being taken.
