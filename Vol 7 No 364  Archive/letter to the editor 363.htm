LETTERS TO THE EDITOR

Destroying to Build

Dear Editor,

 

You do not have to be a civil engineer to figure out why the Addis Abeba road projects are delayed so far. Fortune has published several articles in the past couple of months trying to figure out the problem by interviewing the head of Addis Abeba Roads Authority, Fekade Haile, and a representative of Berta Construction PLC who is involved on two of the projects in the Old Airport area.
 

Fekade admits that "The failure on the part of the Authority to complete the timely removal of the homes and infrastructure lines" is one of the major factors for the delay.  If the owner of the project, Addis Abeba City Roads Authority(AACRA), the consultant and the contractor agree that removing houses, electric, water, and telephone lines is the major factor for the delay, Fortune is obligated to find out what is being done by these public utility companies to solve this major problem that is hindering the progress of the road projects in Addis Abeba.  If the municipality fails to remove houses and relocate utility lines as per the agreement, the owner of the project will be responsible for the delay.
 

The general public including the international community and businesses affected by the road construction should demand an honest answer by these public utility companies including Fortune who is reporting on the case.

Yodit Meshesha
 

World Class Upgrading, Transforming

 

Dear Editor,

In reference to the STORY "MIDROC Caters to Ethiopian" [Volume 7 Number 361, April 3, 2007], in principle Ethiopian Airlines is a strong advocate of private investment. The Airline always supports and actively encourages investments at airports that create additional values and enhance services to customers as well as airline operators.
 

However, with regard to the information in your paper we would like to offer the following clarifications. Ethiopian Airlines sees the catering business and operation as strategic and critical to the success of its main line of business and has no future plan to out source it to other service provides
 

The Airline is currently upgrading and transforming its existing catering facility by involving a world class catering company. Once the facility is upgraded Ethiopian Airlines will start providing services to other airlines within the next six months. It will also be able to accommodate the growing demand expected with the expansion plan in connection to the introduction of the B787 aircraft; starting September 2008.
 

The Airline also is working to construct new state of the art catering facility within the next two years. With all this expansion in catering we do not see ourselves picking up catering in Addis Abeba from any other catering outfit. While the Airports Enterprise is the governing body that decides on the allocation of land for a given purpose, as a major stakeholder in the development of Bole International Airport, Ethiopian Airlines believes allocation of such large plot of land for the venture will definitely constrain the future development of the Airport and apparently compromise Ethiopian Airlines' strategic interest.
 

It is our strong opinion that Fortune should have consulted the concerned officials of the Airline in order to clearly reflect Ethiopian Airlines position rater than publishing information provided by an individual employee that has no clear awareness and first hand information on catering related activities being undertaken by the Airline.

 

Guenet Berhe

Director of Advertising and Promotions, Ethiopian Airlines

 
 

New, Improved Liquid Service

Dear Editor,

The stories headlined "Sugar Centre Moves to New Horizon" [Volume 7 Number 359, March 18, 2007] and "Molasses Moving Goes Private" [Volume 7 Number 360, March 25, 2007], reflect unchecked information and lead readers to the wrong perceptions about the relationships between Djibouti and Ethiopia.
 

The second paragraph of the first story started that the Ethiopian Sugar Industry Support Centre was forced to terminate its agreement with its long term partner Shell Djibouti because of the new system that is being used by the Port.
 

The story asserted that this decision of the Port, not only hurt Shell Djibouti but also the Ethiopian-Djibouti RailWay Enterprise which you say has been forced to stop the services that it has been rendering for over 30 years.
 

In December 2005, it was announced officially, to each party, that the liquid bulk terminals in Djibouti Port would be closed and transferred to Doraleh Port's Horizon Terminal.
 

Indeed the new bulk liquid terminal in Doraleh is today a strategic and economical input for the Ethiopian import-export liquid production and for the export of molasses.
 

The construction of the new fuel Port follows a series of reasons including the heavy underground pollution of the Port and sea by the obsolete equipments and materials of the fuel companies operating in the Port. In fact, the underground of the Port is so polluted today that it will take a long time and a lot of money to clean it.

 

The great danger that the terminals of major oil companies, old tanks and pipelines, and the loading and unloading hazardous cargo from and to vessels was done with all other cargo movement, and transport trucks carrying this cargo were driven through dwelling areas. The limited capacity of the old Port did not allow the use of larger and cheaper vessels with the available draft of tankers and gas carriers. Ethiopia's import-export sector sometimes needed three to four days to unload a vessel for oil products. With Horizon it is only 24 hours, and costs about 30,000 dollars per oil tanker. The Ethiopian Petroleum Enterprise received 50 vessels in 2006.
 

Likewise the Port of Djibouti is growing every day. The vessels are becoming bigger and bigger and the Port follows and develops accordingly. Companies involved in Port activities are growing too, and such change is fair to Ethiopia.
 

Ethiopian liquid traffic is growing fast and its capacity needs are increasing more than 10pc a year, so new facilities are needed. This is why the Port anticipated the expansion, which was not possible at the former site. Horizon Liquid Bulk Terminal, a modern, efficient, computerised and secure facility, has cheaper handling and storage fees than the former one.
 

For molasses, Horizon decreased its rates to include the losses that would be incurred by the railway system and the HDTL calculation was proposed and accepted by ESSIC.
 

Horizon Liquid Bulk Terminal facility offers new opportunities to the Ethiopian market regarding edible oil, to LPG to avoid deforestation, and other products useful for the development of its industries.
 

The Djibouti Government and DP World Investment has put in place the necessary platforms and facilities for smooth and efficient service and operations for the Greater Horn of Africa Region economy.

We hope that the above information is clear and we would like to reiterate once again that we are open for any clarification or information you may require about the Port of Djibouti and Doraleh in the future.


Port of Djibouti
