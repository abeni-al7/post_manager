Punic Victory
 
 

By Lulit Amdemariam

  

 
 

The smell of freedom is in the air with the recent release of some members of the opposition and journalists that have been incarcerated since November 2005. It certainly has been a source of conversation for many, and the hot topic at most gathering places in our fair capital.
 

I was genuinely surprised at the lack of the ‘bada boom bada bang’ sort of ‘I told you so’ reaction that I was expecting from the public in general. Not to say that I was anticipating anything large or even small scale for that matter, but I thought at least there would be some kind of a quiet electric current that you could at least feel if not see and describe.
 

But no, not really at all what happened was it? 62 people that were put on trial have been acquitted or had their charges dropped so far; there were a total of 131 defendants. Still no ‘whoopdy dah’.
 

Granted you have to admit that the main poster children for the opposition and all that it stood for, which I still  have not been able to figure out, are still incarcerated, although they have had their more humorous charges of treason and attempted genocide in favour of merely the charge of trying to overthrow the government.
 

Still, no fireworks, nothing at all. The entire manner with which the issue has thus far been treated does not really catch me off guard. I have to admit, in the back of my mind, I somehow expected that it would happen. I have had the pleasure of attending some of these proceedings and I am amazed that things have even managed to trudge along this far.
 

It has been a long time since the unrest in the country; so many things have happened since then and many of us are just trying to stay afloat on a day to day basis. Having lost none of its importance and symbolism, the trial though, has lost its lustre. I will be the first to admit that I no longer buy a newspaper hoping to find such coverage.

 

Time has dragged on, and the people that were once the centre of our attention are now nowhere to be found, and as the saying goes, out of sight out of mind. So, like many other things in our lives, we take the most recent developments in stride and keep on keeping on so to speak.
 

One has to confess that you can not help but yell a silent ‘yeepee’ on the inside when first hearing the news. I am not a supporter of any given party, although I did, at the time of the elections, prefer the incumbent, purely on the basis that the country was not ready for a power shift because this ideology of democracy had yet to permeate itself into the lives of the people.
 

This is Africa; worse yet, third world Africa; how often do elections go just they way that they are supposed to in this part of the world? Lets be realistic, it just does not happen. People, and this is a nation that has survived Ras Tefferi Mekonnen as King Reagent and Mengistu Hailemariam's Red Terror, were surprised, awed, shocked, appalled, even to the point of reaching devastation, when the accused were thrown in jail. Has that very same history that we are so jealously proud of taught us nothing? Forget the history; has not first hand experience with other governments on this very soil shed any insights or wisdom?
 

What I still have not been able to figure out was why people reacted in the manner that they did. What sort of rose coloured glasses were people looking through when they thought that things would be different and what they wanted to happen would?
 

Have we forgotten where we are as a people and a nation? Enough; that is crying over spilt milk. But the latest turn of events, that is, the release of some of these prisoners, shows that there is hope yet, for this, our ancient and often warped nation. Some semblance of the independence of the judiciary was seen in these releases. Maybe in the bird's eye view of things, the people that were released do not make for as big a display in a trophy case, but they have been released. The judiciary showed as objective a judgement as could be expected from the situation, and I for one, am pleased.

 

Maybe next time caution will be much higher on the agenda as will subtlety and diplomacy, maybe then democracy may step a few centimetres forward.
