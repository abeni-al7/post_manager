Adama’s New Adm’n Faced with Murky Predecessor, Past Mistakes

  
 

 

  
     
 
 















 

  

The newly appointed Adama City Administration Cabinet met with the inactive Development Council of the town, a body that has been stagnant over the last two years. Meeting on Wednesday April 11, 2007 the Cabinet and Council pledged to work closely together. The meeting, the first of its kind attended by members of the Council since its formation two years ago, was a discussion forum for participants to air their grievances.
 

Created after the May 2005 election, following the violence in Adama, the Council was formed under the Special Zone Administration, which existed at the time. With aims to promote investment and development in the area, the body has been practically inactive, given allegations of corruption and power abuse by the newly replaced Administration.
 

In opening remarks at the half day meeting, the new Mayor of Adama, Sisay Negash, pledged to work with the cabinet to remedy and make right the plethora of mistakes that were attributed to the former Cabinet.
 

“Our goal is to establish an Administration that will aid in the rapid development of Adama,” said Sisay.

 

The former members of the Adama Cabinet that were removed from their positions were accused of using biased methods to award plots.

 

“They gave away all the decent and coveted land to people they knew and who were from areas they had connections in,” complained a participant. “They had worn out the patience of locals as well as outside investors that wanted to put their money in the town and its surroundings.”
 

Although this is not the first time that such accusations have been voiced, no charges have been brought against the former Administration’s Cabinet, nor have there been any in depth investigations into the allegations.
 

Another open wound that has scarred Adama town was also a hot issue during the meeting. Genb Gebeya, commonly known as Hamus Gebeya, formerly the largest market in the town, was demolished after the 4,000sqm plot was awarded to Adama Mali SC.
 

The once extremely popular and central market was brought down without hearing the grievances of the tenants, who claimed that they had the ability to develop the area themselves under the Genb Gebeya Businesses SC, a cooperative share company established by vendors of the market seven years ago. According to federal investment policies, priority is supposed to be given to tenants of any plot before outside investors, if they have the ability to develop it themselves. Genb Gebeya had plans to build a three storey mall complex on the plot, designed by MH Engineering PLC.
 

This led to serious civil unrest in the town that needed the assistance of security forces on the day that it was demolished on Saturday, May 27, 2006.
 

The plot now sits empty with a few shacks built from corrugated sheet metal adorning it. The former patrons of the over 2,500 vendors of the market are now forced to shop in all quarters of the town where respective vendors have set up shop.
 

“Transportation costs alone are killing residents, something has to be done about this,” said a participant of the meeting. “People are getting robbed and looted for their goods; it has turned into a haven for thieves.”
 

In addition to having to deal with a weak Administration, Adama, over the last two years since the national elections, has seen a steady deterioration in cleanliness and health facilities.
 

Seyoum partly attributes this problem to the lack of implementation of the Master Plan of the town.
 

“There are over 600 containers and illegally built homes that are contributing to this,” said the new Mayor.
 

He added that using force would not be an option in implementing the Master Plan, but that the Cabinet would use discussions with tenants to clear their plots.
 

“An important and decisive role will be played here by the Council, who is responsible for land related issues,” said Seyoum.
 

In addition to a new Cabinet, the 14 Kebelles of the town have also been appointed new Administrations; the previous Administrations of the respective institutions were removed from their positions and replaced on March 14 and 15, 2007, around the same time as the appointment of the new town Cabinet.
 

Facing the same accusations as the former Cabinet, the new members of the Kebelle Administrations held a week long assessment session in Adama two weeks ago.
 

Broken up into two groups of seven, they met in the Kebelle four and 10 meeting halls, identifying the failures and weaknesses of the previous administration, which they would work to amend, according to a participant of the sessions.
 

Complaints were voiced that people removed from their positions for wrong doing, particularly selling Kebelle homes, transferring Kebelle house titles to individuals, selling off parts of investment plots for personal gain, and putting the development donations of the community to personal use; were simply shifted to a different department.
 

“They retain some sort of job in the Kebelles, which brings no tangible change to the situation,” said a resident of Kebelle four, where one of the meetings was held.
 

The meetings were adjourned to collect evidence against the members of administrations that had complaints lodged against them.

The new Cabinet will begin work officially tomorrow, Monday, April 23, 2007.

 

 

By DANIEL KIFLE
