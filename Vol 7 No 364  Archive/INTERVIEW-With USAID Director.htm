USAID Director

Consciously Bullish 

     
 
 
 

Glenn Anders, mission director of the United States Agency for International Development (USAID) in Ethiopia had a decorated background when he arrived in Ethiopia back in June 2006. He was honoured by the highest medal of Guatemala, the “Orden del Quetzal,” bestowed upon him by the country’s President. The President appreciated Mr. Anders for his “strong commitment and interest in the wellbeing and progress of Guatemala and its people.”

A native of Florida, Mr. Andres is a veteran in the world of development assistance. He has been serving USAID for over 25 years in South East Asia, Latin America, West Africa and now East Africa, after graduating from the University of London in political economy, and obtaining an engineering degree from the University of Virginia. Although his latest experiences were in Latin America, it is clear that he cherishes his years in India, a country he is pleased to see growing impressively.

He would like to see Ethiopia graduate from its poverty trap. And helping Ethiopia through in its efforts not only to boost productivity in agriculture but also in establishing facilities in marketing seems to be his priority. In fact, with the blessing of nature in the past three consecutive years, and the number of people categorized as food insecure dramatically going down in Ethiopia, the Agency he is leading here has drastically shifted its focus: from humanitarian assistance to development cooperation. 

USAID is a federal agency that provides financial, technical and humanitarian assistance to more than 80 countries around the world. For over 40 years, its programmes have contributed to improving the lives of people who live in conditions of vulnerability and poverty. Mr. Anders strongly believes that the sustainable way out of this trap is transforming nations to trade. And helping Ethiopia join the World Trade Organization (WTO) is part of this broader interest of his agency, he told Tamrat G. Giorgis, Fortune’s Managing Editor.
 
 

Fortune: Your Agency, the U.S. government’s arm for international assistance, has been quite prominent in Ethiopia in providing hundreds of millions of dollars in emergency or humanitarian aid over the last 30 years. Lately, however, the number of people that are surveyed food insecure has gone down for the first time to 1.3 million. What new role has USAID in Ethiopia evolved into to address the multiple problems in this country?

 
Mr. Anders: We have shifted from having been largely a humanitarian programme, particularly on food aid to vulnerable groups; there was a series of drought years, a new government, and many problems. Our new administrator, Andrew Natsios, who came in under the Bush Administration, said “let us try to move away.” At the same time, I think this was also very much on the minds of the government because this government is concerned about dependency.
 

Does it mean that you have something you like about this government? 

 

A number of things; nonetheless, it is a tough government.
 

Your budget for Ethiopia has not been reduced (it has been reduced marginally, but still it is around half a billion dollars) although the amount of food aid requirement of the country has reduced dramatically.
 

The food aid actually has gone down. Some years ago, it was 400 million dollars; it is down to 150 million dollars now. It has declined fairly precipitously over the last four years.

Yes, indeed, our total aid package for the last three years has been roughly the same but what has happened is that food aid has declined; meanwhile programmes on HIV/Aids have increased quite dramatically. We have also for the first time started paying a bit more attention to democracy and governance programmes to which we have allotted 10 million dollars. A lot of this is going to conflict mitigation.

 

We have done a lot of work in rural areas - in pastoral as well as ethnic conflicts in the Gambella area, and the border disputes with the Oromia Regional State. We feel that in some communities if we get the elders and religious leaders together they can actually resolve these conflicts more effectively than bringing in government right away at the beginning. The government obviously has a role to play because they need to formalize any agreement.
 

What else are you involved in? I know that you are one of the major parties in supporting these safety-net programmes in rural Ethiopia.
 
The shift from humanitarian emergencies is to a longer-term programme: It has manifested itself in two new directions. One was this productive safety-net programme which is really a multi-donor effort; we helped pilot it with the idea of trying to identify vulnerable families, and set them up with something they can rely on for several years to help them rebuild assets both on community as well as personal levels. By community level, I mean reforesting the water sheds, regaining and terracing. We have seen springs comeback because of this work. On the personal level the assets include buying cattle, chickens, bee hives, and new types of crops. I believe this programme pertains very well for the future, with seven million people covered in rural Ethiopia. Some say it should be eight million.
 

Isn’t this a programme where the international community provides cash for work, instead of food for work as had been practised for many years?

 

Yes, but USAID still provides food for work. We usually have 150 million dollars a year to support this as well as some grants for technical assistance because these communities can rebuild assets but then they also need some ideas on how to generate different sources of income to move away from such subsistence agriculture. That, we think, builds resilience in these communities. It has not really been tested yet because we have three consecutive years with fairly good rainfall; we are hoping that when the next drought comes these communities will be able to handle it because the landscape will be rehabilitated and people will have assets that they can sell off to help them through.
 

The second direction was to start investing more on agricultural productivity. And the way you do that, we believe, is by a market driven approach, less emphasis on production and more on helping people find and meet market requirements. Again this has really only been two years old, although our initial indications are very good. I think we have helped cooperatives understand marketing of their goods, working perhaps on up to six sub sectors: livestock; skins and hides, related to that is meat; finished leather and leather goods; coffee; and finally floriculture.
 

Since you are working in food security and rural safety-nets, I would hope that your Agency follows the dynamics of the demand and supply of grains. What has been seen in the past year is a record high of agricultural productivity (over 150 million quintals if we go by the government claims), oddly coupled with an increase in prices. As a result, there are some people who doubt whether the production estimate is real.
 

There are two studies by the World Bank and the [British] Department for International Development (DfID) with hard and empirical conclusions on this. My sense after talking to a lot of people is that perhaps production has not quite gone up as much as the government thinks; but we know it has gone up. After three good years, farmers have a lot more income, thus they are in a position to consume more now than they were selling because they feel they have more income.

There has also been a bit of a boom in the construction sector; both road construction in rural areas and secondary cities. That has contributed to more income. The safety-net programme also brings some income. At the same time, there is a bit of an increase in cash crops. I think the market is working pretty well. It is a question of supply, which is growing this much, but demand is growing much faster than the supply is. I think it is as simple as that.
 

Do you have any programme to mitigate inflation that is pressuring the urban poor with fixed incomes?

 

Back in November, I had a discussion with the Deputy Prime Minister [Addisu Legesse] about the problem and he was quite concerned about it. He asserted that there was some way that we could do a government to government grant of grain in order for them to make some intervention on the market. They have an emergency reserve that they cannot use unless they have a guarantee of more grains coming in.
 

It would be very expensive for the government to buy from the market. Although it took us some time because there is no tradition of a government to government grain programme in Ethiopia, finally we have agreed to provide a six million dollars guarantee for 19,000tns of grain. They can use this amount to stabilize and hold down grain prices on the urban market. The feeling is that if you get a bit of grain out there, even on a temporary surplus, it could be used as a price signal to anyone in a rural area that is holding grain that there is a bit of a downturn and that they better sell to the market.
 

I see that USAID is now shifting focus from its, I would say, traditional area of assistance to helping the private sector. Three years ago, your budget to do anything with the private sector was about 200,000 dollars, which has grown to 17 million dollars now; you have a lot more activities in the private sector. Is this due to the compassion fatigue emerging in the West that people are simply tired of supporting Ethiopia forever? Or is it because the need on the ground has changed radically? What is this about?
 

First, it is unique to Ethiopia that we just began working with the private sector. This was the case because we were always on the humanitarian cycle and there was so much emergency aid, for instance, in humanitarian assistance and worrying about water and food shortages. Second, the government was not terribly interested in that at the time.
 

Let’s be frank, the government took a very strong hand and there was really little opportunity for the private sector here in the last decade. Nonetheless, we have a long history of working with the private sector (since the 1980s) in Central America where our support was instrumental in changing the pattern of the countries from subsistence farming to large varieties of imports and exports of vegetables both between the countries and to the United States, as well as the Caribbean. That was not the case in Ethiopia before; as Ishac Diwan [World Bank Director to Ethiopia and Sudan] told you a few weeks ago that he is modestly satisfied, I am being consciously bullish.

 

But isn’t it true that there exists this compassion fatigue in the West?

 

I think there is a feeling, however that our assistance to Africa has increased three or four fold in the last several years.
 

But not enough to represent 0.7pc of your GDP as the developed world has agreed to make available?
 

We do not look at numbers like that. A lot of our assistances do not come through the government. It comes through private channels; we have a tremendous line of private humanitarian or development assistance that comes to developing countries. For instance, I met a group of Americans here who are funding, privately, several hundred thousand dollars in water projects in Semera and Asayta, in Afar Regional State.

I do not think that we have agreed to that number [0.7pc of GDP]. We said that we will increase our share but I do not think we want to put a number on it. I think it is realistic because all our executives, thus the head of the USAID, could not have agreed to a number like that because it is the Congress that appropriates money in our system.

 

Despite the surge in your funding to the private sector over the past two years, the amount, 17 million dollars, still represents a fraction of the 450 million dollars operation you are undertaking in Ethiopia. Any possibility that you may beef this up in the coming years?
 

Indeed, it is a small fraction, but again that depends on Congress and how it appropriates money to us. We try to allocate it worldwide but Ethiopia will always have an important claim on it because of the safely-net programme and the size of the population in [humanitarian] need. There is also a very strong feeling in our Congress about health, child survival and maternal care, thus Congress sets certain amounts of money that we have to spend globally on health issues. Once again, given the poor health indicators in rural areas, the size of the population will always get a large part of the earmarked fund. There is also the Presidential HIV/Aids initiative which is growing to be quite large. Neither I nor the Ambassador or our office in Washington D.C. has the discretion to make such large shifts in our portfolio.
 

There are too many reasons why we do not need lots of money in areas of democracy and governance, as well as support to the private sector. You [the private sector] are not building roads, nor are you going to expand the whole health or education system. Often, it is a matter of ideas or seed money, and sometimes you can have a tremendous impact on a business just by sending somebody to markets where there are trade shows.
 

The investments do not have to be that large. It is really a matter of exchanging ideas and stimulating people; after all, we cannot give money to the private sector. We try to bring an idea, better certification, grading and standards, and demonstrate to people what the market wants and what they need to produce.
 

Nevertheless, next year is going to be more where we expect to go towards 30 million dollars for general economic growth.

 
 

If you were to scale-up your support to the private sector next year, what is your personal impression about the level of entrepreneurship in this country? Do you find the private sector aggressive enough to take advantage of the availability of this fund?
 
It [entrepreneurship] is still low; there is a need for more business administration trainings in order to make people more entrepreneurial. What you need is people who perhaps have either a secondary or basic university education and give them business skills or show them the opportunities. It has only been five years since a growing awareness on the part of the government exists to start supporting the private sector. This is just the beginning in Ethiopia.
 

Let me take you to another issue which I think USAID is very passionate about. It is supporting Ethiopia, technically, to improve its negotiating capacity in its bid to join the World Trade Organization (WTO). Are you satisfied with the responsiveness of the Ethiopian government in that aspect?
 

Well, there were two years while it lay dormant.

 

I am aware that you had brought a top-notch trade lawyer from Washington D.C. He stayed here for those two years and nothing happened. He went back and landed a job as a commissioner in the U.S. trade bureau.
 

When he left, there was a draft memo. He conducted a series of trainings while he was here with the Ministry of Trade and Industry; he built a lot of capacity and when he left the draft memo on the foreign trade regime was close to finalization.
 

People say he had left out of frustration.
 

Not really, he left because he got a much better job. Two years in the life of a government is not a terribly long time; understandably it could be a long time in the life of an individual.
 

We started our programme just a year ago once we thought there was willingness on the part of the government and after the Minister for Trade and Industry, Girma Birru, demonstrated his strong interest. We have had courses since then on geographical designations, on trade marking and on negotiating trade agreements. We had a request from the highest level in the Prime Minister’s Office for specific types of assistance on trade negotiations. We have also been helping in organizing trips by high level officials to Geneva, Switzerland, so that they can talk to the WTO about what will be required.
 

In the last six months I have been here, I was working closely with the Ministry of Trade and Industry, particularly with State Minister Tadesse Haile, and I have seen keen interest at the very highest levels of Government. We do not provide assistance unless we think there is an interest and willingness to accept it.
 

Of the 150 member countries of the WTO, it is your country that seems exceptionally interested in helping Ethiopia in the accession process. I cannot help but wonder why?

 

It is not accession to the WTO that is important to us. It is the reforms that go with it to open the country to global competition.
 

Why should the US Government care about this?
 

Our development philosophy is that trading nations develop faster. And we are a development agency. We want Ethiopia to develop as quickly as possible. We think it would increase its trade; but it will always be victimized in global markets if it does not have WTO protection as well as open its own market somewhat. It is a quid pro quo and you have to negotiate that carefully.

 

Is it out of your ideological conviction that a country would develop if it trades more or is it because the US Government is specifically interested in the openings of some sectors, especially the telecom industry?
 

I do not believe it is just ideological; I think it is empirically proven that the countries that trade develop much faster. Even countries that are closed and have undemocratic regimes like China experience this. India used to be a closed economy, look at the double-digit growth now. I worked in India. I never would have imagined that we would see this type of growth. Again it is because India is becoming a trading nation. Our interest is to see Ethiopia develop fast. It needs accelerated economic growth to do that.
 

The process of WTO accession involves our [US] office of trade representatives; USAID is not part of that at all. When Ethiopia’s application goes to the WTO, it will be an issue to our trade representative’s office, which is very keen about these types of issues. What is important for us [at the USAID] is that Ethiopia becomes an active trading partner in a global economy. And whether it has to open its telecommunication sector is going to be a matter of negotiations at very high levels. We are trying to help Ethiopia understand how these negotiations work and what type of information and negotiating skill would be necessary.

 

But, judging from the series of speeches made by your subsequent ambassadors emphasising the need to open the telecom sector . . . .
 

The ambassadors have a broader agenda. They have several interests; one is in opening markets for US businesses. Obviously, we are delighted, and the Ambassador certainly is very happy that Ethiopia has chosen Boeing as its airline because this represents tremendous amounts of sales for the US.
 

Nevertheless, whether it has an ideological, theoretical, or economic basis, I would argue that opening the telecommunications sector with regulation and taxing it would be better for this country than trying to maintain state ownership. I am not arguing that because it is good for US investment. That is another part of our government that worries about what is good for US investors, or what kind of good trade deals we can work out.
 

That is not USAID’s job. Our job is to try to assist this country achieve broader economic growth. We feel one way to do that is to promote trade. And if you want to trade, you need to be part of the world trading system, of which the WTO is referee.
 

Let me take you back to what happened a year and half ago. Many of the members in the Development Assistance Group (DAG) in Ethiopia suspended direct budget support following the May 2005 electoral violence. The US Government and USAID, which is a member of the DAG, were kind of quiet about all those issues, allegedly having an overriding interest in fighting terrorism. As a result, the United States seems to be quite unpopular in the eyes of people here and fall under criticism by the Diaspora in the West. What do you personally feel about all this?
 
That is a whole other set of political issues that I am not an expert in. I was not here in 2005 but I think we did have an observer group here during the election, including President Carter himself, and we felt that the opposition had lost the election. While there were a number of expressions of concern, and condemnation for the killings that occurred, we think some of our other donor partners had gone over board in encouraging the opposition and making them think that somehow they had won the election; we never believed that.
 

Are you referring to the European Union?
 

Again, I do not know the specifics. But, I know that there were some European countries that seem to be of the thought that the opposition had won, which in fact they had not. Yes, they won some seats in some areas, while there were some contested seats.
 

We did not take the strong position that was [taken] by the Europeans. We feel that while the crackdown after the election was unnecessarily tough, the government did win the elections and the opposition should have come to terms with that and try to work out better arrangements with the government.
 

We have a long history with Ethiopia and we just did not think that what happened was enough of a tragedy that we should somehow break our engagement with the government. I think in the end it worked. We also did not have budgetary support; there was nothing for us to cut off anyway. We certainly will not stop our food aid because that will hurt the people.
