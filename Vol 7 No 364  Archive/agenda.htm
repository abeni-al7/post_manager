Feeding Poor  Wheat

Taxing Luxurious Consumption.
 

 

  
 

Seifu Hailu is a resident of the Bole District's Kebelle 17, father of five and works as a daily labourer. He is the sole bread winner for his pregnant wife and their five children, but the recent slow in the construction sector has made feeding his family difficult.

 

"Prices have gone up, there are times when there is no work because of the lack of construction materials, I worry about how I am going to put food in the mouths of my children," Seifu told Fortune.

 

But the recent decision by the government to provide low priced grain to the middle and lower income sections of the capital has been a Godsend for Seifu.
 

"When I heard the announcement, it was as though my prayers had been answered; at least that is one less thing to worry about," he said.
 

Seifu is one of the millions that are going to benefit from the government's benevolence, although others are being impacted in a different manner.
 

The hot topic this week, particularly for the Addis Abeba import happy community, is the announcement by minister of Finance and Economic Development, Sufain Ahmed, on Wednesday, April 11, 2007, of the government's imposition of a surtax.
 

The special tax that is levied against a certain class, or as in this case, certain goods, was stated to be a method to finance the recent decision that the government made to inject grain into the local market in order to stabilise the booming prices.
 

The rise in the cost of living has been a serious challenge, particularly in Addis Abeba and other urban areas of the country, where some statistics put unemployment rates at 45pc. The urban middle and lower classes have not been able to increase their standards of living incrementally with the increases in cost of basic commodities like fuel, grains, vegetables and rent. There have been wide discrepancies between supply and demand in this fledgling market economy.
 

Realising the challenge that such populations have been having difficulty in overcoming, the government has decided to purchase grain and use some of its reserves to subsidise the grain market in order to prop up these facets of society.
 

It will make the grain available at the district level, with those that do not have immediate access to cash able to use coupons to have access to the grain.

 

The Prime Minister, along with agricultural, rural and economic experts, have partly attributed the increase in prices to the increase in the purchasing power of the farmer and, as a result, the ability to hoard agricultural products in anticipation of better prices and a higher profit.

 

"We can not force the farmer to sell his stock," said Prime Minister Meles Zenawi during his six-month report to the House of People's Representatives three weeks ago.
 

And indeed, in the free market system, the government can not. But the dire situation of the urban middle and lower classes has dictated and even necessitated that the government intervene in some way before larger social repercussions arise.
 

The government has reached the decision to inject wheat at low prices using the District and Kebelle networks to ration the commodity to their residents. However, there are a series of setbacks that come hand in hand with intervention rationing. It has huge administrative costs and is more susceptible to corruption because of the size of the network required.
 

But purchasing the additional grain needed to inject into the market, as well as to replenish the stocks that it is going to be depleting, requires funding. Subsidies do not come cheap, and since the intervention is merely a temporary one, the government also has to find a temporary means of income to supplement it.

 

In order to strengthen its financial capacity without affecting the sections of the society that it is trying to help, the imposition of  10pc surtax on imported luxury items is expected to wield the government hundreds of millions in revenue while not effecting investment activities.

 

The tax will only include limited products which are not related with basic needs such as cloths, packed foods, electronics, beverages and perfumes.
 

Investment goods, petroleum and lubricants, public and freight transports and special purpose motor vehicles, certain medicines, aircraft, spacecraft and their parts are excluded from the surtax.

But the implementation process has been seeing some glitches, with soap producers in the country having the Ethiopian Customs Authority charging surtax on their raw materials. Businessmen in the sector, 10 in number joined forces and wrote a letter to the Ministry of Finance and Economic Development (MoFED) asking that the surtax on their raw materials be lifted, because it defeats the purpose of the imposition in the first place.

 

"Because the soap sector employs thousands of the middle and lower income groups and its product is vital to the day to day lives of Ethiopians, placing an additional tax on the raw materials would affect the very facets of society that the surtax is trying to aid," said the letter dated April 17, 2007.

 

Statistics indicate that low and medium income earners spend 60pc of their income on consumption goods and 10-15pc on housing; about 50pc of that consumption is on food. These two are not affected by the surtax, thereby exempting the majority of urban populations from the burden that the higher echelons are dealt.
 

Haile Kibret (PhD), head of the Macroeconomic Section at the Ethiopian Economic Association, told Fortune that if the newly imposed tax is used for its designed objective, to increase earnings and subsidise the urban poor, then it is a positive move.
 

"As wonderful and appreciable as that may be, there are three factors that need to be known, such as the amount of revenue collected, the size of the population subsidised, and the type of goods included in the tariff; in other words, we need to know the measure of responsiveness of the taxed commodities" said Haile.
 

He explained that if the commodity was inelastic, yes, the government could earn the projected amount of money. A commodity is defined as being inelastic when price fluctuations do not determine the number of consumers demanding the good.
 

"In this case, there are a lot of commodities that the surtax is levied on, some elastic and some inelastic, so we can not exactly project how much the government is going to earn from this endeavour," he said.
 

Projecting a vague 'in hundreds of millions' from surtax earnings, whatever the amount earned, it is ultimately taking from the rich to subsidise the poor.
 

"As gallant as a concept as this is, this sort of system will not be effective because, whenever there is the imposition of a tariff, there are also increases in general price levels," said Haile. "The only plausible and long term solution for this problem is to find out the sources of the inflation, is it wages, policies; once the source has been identified, attacking the problem will not be difficult because you know exactly where it comes from."
 

For the short term, the paths that the government has chosen may play a role in easing the markets somewhat, but there are also important factors that must be given due attention if the system is to work. Time is the most important parameter that needs close thought; the longer the period of intervention, the deeper the distortion it will cause in the market.
 

A secure and transparent transportation system is needed for the distribution of the grain. If transportation is not given careful consideration, the government may end up accomplishing exactly the opposite of what it is intending, helping the rich by granting costly contracts and hurting the poor further, failing to pay for the subsidy.
 

In addition, supplementary tariffs often lead to an increase in smuggling, so as to avoid that in distributor prices. It is contingent upon the government to increase security in areas known for smuggling so as to minimise the effects on projected earnings.
 

This is not the first time that surtax has been imposed in the country; it was also implemented in 1999 by Proclamation 59/1999 by the Council of Ministers during the Ethio-Eritrean war, but in that instance to supplement military spending.
 

At the recent press conference, given only to the public media, Sufian pointed out that the tax was not permanent, but only a short term strategy that would cease once the grain markets had been stabilised.
 

Meanwhile there is another unnamed advantage, as domestic producers will be given an advantage over foreign producers facing a tax. Though not mentioned by policy makers, this is another aspect, domestic industry protection, intended or not, that accrues as a result of the policy
 

Other important aspects pertaining to success potential remain unclear, according to an economics expert that asked to have his name withheld.
 

"Because of high administrative costs and public misuse, surtax may not be effective," said the expert.

But successful implementation is not the only thing that is brought into question.
 

"This is not the most favourable time for the government to be entering into the market like this," said the expert. "They should at least wait until the harvest season, so at least they have some leverage. For now it does not make a difference to the farmers that are not selling their product."
 

Interestingly enough, with all this talk about decreasing the prices of grain, the government is focusing on one brand: wheat. The majority of the price increases are seen on locally produced grains that are consumed daily by the population, such as teff and berbere. The reasoning is that seeing a dramatic decrease in wheat prices consumers may substitute into wheat consumption, lowering the demand, and thus price, of local grains.
 

"We can not import teff and berbere, they are not available on the international market," said Meles during the question and answer session.
 

The government logic is to reduce inflation by increasing wheat supply, funding the endeavour by taxing only the wealthy.
 

"Higher income groups can afford it; rationally speaking, the current duties that are imposed on the items that have had a surtax added to them are extremely high by anyone's standards," added the expert.

Surtax is practiced in many free market economies, for example, in Sweden it is levied on alcohol as no economy is free from any tax of some sort.
