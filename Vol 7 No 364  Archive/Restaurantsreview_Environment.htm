Environment      

One thing anyone could say about the JOI is that one feels that it could be a typical traditional restaurant in India, without having the touristy aspects of trying to overly promote the culture. Although of course one would want to experience the culture and traditions as much as one would the dishes. Diners will feel the coziness of the close-quartered restaurant with its nine tables in the main dining area and its few more others in the non-smoking area.

 

The restaurant during the day appears a bit dark, which is further accentuated by the deep burgundy seats, white table cloths topped with burgundy ones, burgundy napkins and a dark wooden threshold marking the different sections of the main eating room.
 

The curtains at the restaurant are white, as our the walls, on which framed drawings of Indian women hang, and above the chimney, an array of wine bottles line its top.
 

The tables are already set when you enter, with clean glassware and cutlery of no special feature, placed neatly for use. At the center, a white vase with a carnation and a rose, decorate the room in their brightness.
 

Some Indian music plays in the background at a very low volume, and appears not to be crowded during lunch hours as well as dinner, making it a good spot for business luncheons as well as a romantic setting for a couple.
 

Despite the fact that the lit candles on the table, which could have been romantic, somehow seem unnecessary considering the brightly lit atmosphere, couples could still find the coziness to their comfort.
