NBE, charged with controlling the monetary aspects of the Ethiopian economy, failed to grant access to government bank accounts twice in the past week, as power failures left officials panicking. Allegations of data loss and chaos led to general concerns over the efficacy of the current electrical system, for which the Bank paid over two million Birr, that has led to a continuation of the problems in the current building that began in the 1990s.
 

  
 

NBE Power Outage Freezes Gov’t Accounts

  
 

 

  
     
 
 















 

  

Power outages at the National Bank of Ethiopia (NBE), twice in the last two weeks, have made the bank accounts of government institutions, including the Office of the Prime Minister, inaccessible, sources disclosed to Fortune.
 

The sources also added that data from the Bank’s single server was lost as a result of the outages.
 

Located on Sudan Street, on the route that leads from the Ministry of National Defence (MoND) to the Ministry of Health (MoH), the building that houses the Central Bank was marred with problems and controversies since the inception of its idea in the early 1990s.
 

To add insult to injury, the electrical system, installed by Siemens at a cost of over two million Birr, is creating problems of recurring power loss.

 

The 11-storey building houses the offices of the Vice Governor of NBE, Alemseged Assefa; Finance and Information Services Directorate; Banking and Foreign Exchange Directorate; and Human Resource and Administration Directorate.
 

During a power outage, the main server of the Bank, which administers the entire network and is monitored by the Finance and Information Service Directorate, went off service, crippling the entire building and its work processes.
 

The Office of the Prime Minister, the Palace Administration, MoND, the Ministry of Finance and Economic Development (MoFED), the Ethiopian Electric Power Corporation (EEPCo) and other large government institutions have domestic and foreign accounts at the Bank. The private sector and individuals do not bank there.

 

“April 17, 2007, in particular was hectic; we could not access our accounts, and getting the electrical problem remedied took longer than usual,” said a MoFED source.

 

The question here is not really the power outages, but rather their effects.

 

A senior NBE official told Fortune, “Yes, it is true that we have had a couple of outages, but these allegations that data has been lost and that customers are being made to suffer endlessly is exaggerated.”
 

Because the telephone line that is used by NBE is connected to the entire network, it also saw a failure during the power outages in the last two weeks. NBE uses a telephone system called IP that is identical to the technology used by the World Bank (WB).

 

“NBE could not even make domestic calls, it was like it was in the dark ages,” said the source.

 

But a major reason that this has been a problem for NBE is because the Central Bank does not have a disaster recovery system.
 

“Though a decision was made to install an additional server that carries a backlog of data in times of emergency, it has yet to be implemented,” said the source. The only server that is being used by NBE now is supplied by GCS/ NCR.
 

Fortune’s repeated attempt to solicit comments from the IT Department Head, Tesfaye Teshome and the Network Administration Head Assefa Yeshanew regarding last week’s failure were unsuccessful, with the two refusing to comment on the situation because they did not have the mandate to disclose information to external institutions.
 

NBE’s headquarters were originally designed for its Banking and Insurance Institute when construction was launched in 1994. Passing a decision to award the Institute a plot in the Akaki Kaliti District, NBE saw the estimated three million Birr costs balloon to over 80 million Br when the building was completed 11 years later in 2005.

 

Seleshi Consulting Architects and Engineers PLC, which did the design and supervision work, has yet to be paid five million Birr for rendered services. The contractor, Berta Construction PLC, also has claimed around 4.5 million Br that the Bank has not paid.

“This Bank shoulders the huge responsibility of controlling the country’s economy; it should take more precautions with these kinds of systems, because, in the long run, it is not hurting customers but government institutions,” said the source.
