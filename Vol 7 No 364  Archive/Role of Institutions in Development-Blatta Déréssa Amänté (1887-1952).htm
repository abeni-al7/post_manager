Role of Institutions in Development: Blatta Déréssa Amänté (1887-1952)

  
 

 

  
     
 
 















 

  

Blatta Déréssa was one the prolific development economists of early 20th century Ethiopia. According to Bahru, Blatta Déréssa had neither gone abroad for education nor attended any of the celebrated educational institutions (traditional or modern) in Ethiopia. He was the member of the Léqa Nägamté aristocracy (Wellega, Southwestern Ethiopia), being cousin of the local ruler Däjjach Kumsa (Gäbrä-Egizbhér) Moroda. In the 1920s he emerged as one of the creative contributors to the weekly BS, which, as noted above, was the intellectual mouth piece of the time.
 

Blatta Déréssa combined intellectual pursuit with exceptional entrepreneur drive. In 1930 he was given the rank of Blatta - a title given to an educated person at the rank of a Director, (having been a fitawrari until then), and made Director in the Ministry of Agriculture. In exile in the Sudan during the brief Italian occupation, he pursued entrepreneurial endeavours. After 1941, Blatta Déréssa developed a reputation as an Oromo oral historian and wrote on the importance of Japan's experience as a model for Ethiopia's development. He characterised or termed this model as 'safe modernisation' and emphasised the significance of institutions (which he also equated with organisation) for development. He also made an impressive analysis of inflation in Ethiopia of that time.

 

Déréssa's emphasis on the role of institutions in development comes from his quest for understanding why ordinary people and government appointees do not shoulder the responsibility of working for their country. He observed that these people invariably leave that responsibility to the government. According to him the main reason for this culture of Ethiopian's is lack of institutions (organisation). He defines organisation as government rules and regulations that specify the task, responsibility, accountability procedure and autonomy of the government appointees in executing the task in question. He noted that this is widely used in Europe and Ethiopia needs to adopt it.
 

Writing in 1926/1927 he noted that for the last 20 years there were a lot of good intentions and efforts towards development (buildings such as the planned bridge over the Nile River, establishing an armament factory, tannery, school for the clergy, modern schools, hospital etc.). However, these efforts were not accompanied by institutional building and resulted in failure to realise these planned targets. Déréssa noted that it is difficult to identify those responsible for this failure because there is no institutional set-up, with relevant accountability, to help us finger point.

 

One way of addressing this, according to him, is sending students to Europe to study organisation. However, he noted, the number of students Ethiopia was sending to Europe during that time was in the range of 10 to 20 people, which he argued was too few to bring meaningful change to a company, let alone a country the size of Ethiopia. He advised the government to follow the footsteps of Japan which sent thousands of people to Europe for education and wrote that the financial burden is not too much, for it can be financed, according to his calculations, by a one month tax income of the Dire Dawa and Addis Abeba customs offices.
 

He stressed the role of institutions by insisting that Ethiopia can not use the old system of running its affairs to run the modern economy (He noted that this is tantamount to a refusal by a gold merchant who used to have a very small scale for weighing the gold he sells and insists on using that when he changes his business to trade in bulky goods such as ivory and wax where a very big scale is needed).
 

His idea of learning from Japan was exceptionally strong when he discussed the role of education in development. Like that of Gebre-Hiewot and Mikael, Déréssa was enthusiastic about the role of education in development and began a fund raising campaign to build schools across the country.

 

For Déréssa, learning about Japan is important not only to make Ethiopians feel guilty of the time they wasted thus far, but also to inspire them to work hard in the future. After giving detailed figures about the Japanese success in education (such as the number of schools, universities, students, student sex ratio, printing presses, and government income) he wrote, Japan (which was 60 years back, as backward as Ethiopia was) is truly independent and rich because of education and hard work towards attaining it. To realise this he suggested setting up a council of wise men that will advise the government on issues of modern education.
 

Déréssa  has also made a link between his idea of organisation (institution) and his utmost determinant of development - education - by illustrating it using the expansion of modern education not only in Japan but also in some of the European colonies of the time (such as Tunisia and Algeria). His insightful analysis began when he examined the issue of how to finance this modern education.

 

Here, he offered probably the first analysis of what economists today call the vicious cycle of poverty, and most interestingly, how to break out of it. Notwithstanding this, the development economics literature attributes the idea of the vicious circle of poverty to the post-Second World War works of Rosenstein-Rodan and Nurkse.

 

Déréssa's vicious cycle of poverty basically states that poverty leads to lack of capacity to expand education which in turn leads to lack of education and the latter to poverty. The solution to this vicious circle of poverty is analogous to the treatment of a sick person, where a physician, who, knowing that his patient is weak, gives him medicine (or carries out a surgery) that will make him even weaker, but eventually better. By the same analogy, Déréssa emphasised the need to make sacrifice (in terms of higher education tax or effectively domestic saving) to expand education across the country which needs to be institutionalised, and the institutions need to have a council of advisors.
 

The final thread that Déréssa discussed relates to the role of the external sector and inflation in Ethiopia's development. In one of his articles, he attempted to resolve the apparent trade-off between rural and urban welfare following a surge in export of agricultural output (live animals) from Ethiopia in 1920s (The trade-off occurred because rural households got higher prices for their produce owing to exports while urban dwellers paid higher prices owing to the shortage in the domestic market induced by the rising level of exports.). He basically argued that such a trade-off is inevitable but could be avoided through rural-urban integration (cooperative) effort that needs to aim at building infrastructure linking the rural and urban areas. This will in turn help both the rural and urban populations to benefit from the export trade by generating a price level between the current export price and the hitherto farm-gate price.
 

The final issue that he examined is inflation, both from global and national perspectives in 1831, the period of the Great Depression in the industrial world.
 

Déréssa made an impressive analysis of inflation in Ethiopia during this period as he stated that hoarding (of the then commodity money) is common in many countries and is a problem created by rich people. This hoarding reduces the amount of money in circulation (hence excess of the demand for money for transaction purpose over money in circulation). This, according to Déréssa, is aggravated by over production and the use of capital intensive technology in industrialised countries that led to losses and unemployment which further reduced the effective demand and hence inflation (compare this to the two famous world economists of the time, Keynes of Britain and Kalecki  of Poland). He noted that this global condition had repercussions for Ethiopia.
 

He said inflation problems in Ethiopia came from a decline in the world price of silver (the commodity money in Ethiopia of that time); since Ethiopian currency was not backed by gold, its value declined in tandem with world price of silver. This has led to depreciation of Ethiopian currency since Ethiopians needed nearly double what they used to require for the same items that they import. This led to concentration of currency in the hands of few merchants leading to a shortage of money in rural areas and, aggravated by a decline in the price of Ethiopia's exports, further reducing the money supply in Ethiopia. These two are the external dimension of the inflation problem in Ethiopia.

 

He then went on discussing what he calls the 'internal cause of inflation' that is created by the Ethiopians themselves. He said, "Today our spending has increased nearly four fold. Half of this is due to fall in the world price of silver. The other half is due to Ethiopians' dual pattern of consumption: the spending required maintaining the age-old Ethiopian traditional pattern of consumption, and the other which is needed for that part of consumption and life style adopted from the Europeans."

 

This pattern, he noted, is also apparent at the level of government structure. Thus, Ethiopians' are spending an amount enough to run two countries, while their source of income is the limited resources of a single country - Ethiopia.

 

This problem could be addressed not by failing to adopt the European model of development but rather by a real understanding of the meaning of development; not dependence on Europe, but rather an expansion of education, especially in areas of industry, developing a saving culture, and hard work. Until we reach the European level, he argued, we can follow as agricultural development strategy in the short run. This needs to be accompanied by reducing imports and consumption of luxuries.

His writings on these subjects basically follow Gebre-Hiwot's line as he had read and was convinced by Gebre-Hiwot's line of thinking about the role of education and the development strategy that Ethiopia needed to follow (The latter is especially apparent in Déréssa's computation of the balance of trade and terms of trade deterioration, which for the first time in Ethiopia was done by Gebre-Hiwot).
