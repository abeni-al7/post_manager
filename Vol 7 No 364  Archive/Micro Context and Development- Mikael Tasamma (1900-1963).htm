Micro Context and Development: Mikael Tasamma (1900-1963)

  
 

 

  
     
 
 















 

  

According to Bahru's excellent book, "Pioneers of Change", in early the 20th century Teffari Mekonnen, at the instigation of Dajjanch Gabra-Sellase Bary-Gabr, the governor of Adwa, sent four students to Italy. One of these students managed to get a distinguished carrier, Mikael Tasamma, a native of Adwa, Tigray and protégé of Dajjach Gabra-Sellase. He went to Hamasen, Eritrea and joined the evangelical school. After his few years stay in Eritrea and first exposition to modern education he moved to Addis Abeba with the help of Dajjach Gabra-Sellase. Subsequently, he went to the University of Rome and wrote a doctoral thesis in political science, but the thesis was deemed anti-Italian and he was not allowed to defend it.

 

He returned to Ethiopia on the eve of the war with Italy and was put in charge of Italian affairs at the Foreign Ministry. During the 'Grazaiani Massacre' (Graziani was a member of the fascist Italian government appointed to 'govern' Ethiopia. He massacred thousands of children and adults in Addis Abeba, following an assassination attempt on his life by two patriot Ethiopians of Eritrean origin) Mikael managed to escape from an Italian concentration camp and saved his life.
 

After the defeat of Italy he occupied the post of Director General and Inspector General at the Ministry of Justice. He retired from government in 1947/1948 and engaged in private legal practice until his death in 1963.

 

Mikael was an active contributor to the economic debate and discussion (in particular on microeconomic issues) in BS. Although Mikael writes on microeconomic issues, including perhaps the first diagrammatic analysis of marginal product and revenue schedules (tools of modern economics) in the Amharic language, he often began his discourse by asking his fellow intellectuals 'why is Ethiopia underdeveloped?', and 'why did Ethiopia fail to expand education, which is vital for development?'. In his search for answers to these questions Mikael refers to the writings of Gebre-Hiwot and Déréssa and concurs with their views - hence the similarity of ideas among these three development economists.
 

Mikael's writings are different from the other two because he makes reference to classic works such as Adam Smith and Italian economists, uses large amounts of data, draws lessons from world history and/or specific countries such as the Netherlands, England, and the US and finally, he offers detailed treatment of economic concepts (invariably accompanied by illustrative examples, including diagrams). He offered an elaborative discussion of, inter alia, wealth creation, distribution of income, exchange, diminishing marginal utility, cost-value-price division of labour, interest rate, profit, business organisation such as cartels and stages of development.

 

Mikael's detailed exposition of economic concepts adds a different dimension to some of Gebre-Hiwot's analysis whose exposition is informed by the labour theory of value. For Mikael, for instance, productive labour could include services and value is not only labour, but also the ability to render utility and the fact that the product is demanded. Like Gebre-Hiwot, however, he believes interest rate is inversely related to the level of development due to a decline in marginal product of capital.
 

Mikael discussed Ethiopian development issues as a digression from his microeconomic exposition. Whenever he did that, development for him comes from hard work and knowledge acquired through education, not from, say, natural resources endowment as such. Like that of Gebre-Hiwot and Déréssa, the role of education in this process is central for Mikael.
 

He believes in the importance of 'self interest' for development and noted that factors of production such as labour and capital get their just reward through the operation of the free market. Contrary to Gebre-Hiewot's concern about distribution of income, Mikael is against a collectivist (or state-based) notion of distribution of income because he believed that such a notion of distribution is not only impractical but also leads to under-development. The only thing that he recommends in relation to this is the need to organise consumer associations, which he inferred from his discussion of industrial organisation in Europe as he noted how vital such associations are in defending the interest of its members.
 

On the external dimension of development, again in contrast to Gebre-Hiwot and Déréssa, he uncritically accepts free world trade as a means to an expansion of world wealth, and from which every country could potentially benefit. He believed international trade could lead to global development and also possibly to a global state under the auspices of which each individual country could prosper.
 

One of the areas where he discussed the issue of Ethiopia's underdevelopment at length is in his account of, perhaps pre-dating the famous American economist Rostow, 'stages of development' and the crucial role of industrialisation to achieve the highest of such stages. Mikael noted that he was forced to write about this issue to encourage Ethiopians to purse industrialisation. However, he wrote, Ethiopians generally wish to industrialise but fail to materialise it. He says, we are slow to act and do not know the value of time; and queries: 'my fellow Ethiopians, when will you be awake from your long sleep?'
 

Readings of Mikael's articles show that he had the intention of teaching his fellow intellectuals and citizens what he calls political economy (which essentially is microeconomics). Although he discussed, rigorously, crucial economic concepts at the micro level, supported by enumerable illustrations and relevant references, his attempt at linking that with Ethiopia's problem were weak, especially by the standard of Gebre-Hiwot and Déréssa.  As aptly remarked by Bahru, 'Mikael's writings look as a faithful translation of Italian [and some of the classical] works and his contribution is pale compared to that of Gebre-Hiwot.'
