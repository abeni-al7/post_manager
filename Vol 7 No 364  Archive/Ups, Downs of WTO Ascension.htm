Ups, Downs of WTO Ascension

 
 

 

  
     
 
 















 

  

An assessment of the Ethiopian financial service sector with respect to the General Agreement on Trade in Services (GATS) was presented at a validation workshop on the impact of WTO Accession held at the Addis Hilton by the Ministry of Trade and Industry (MoTI) on April 20, 2007.
 

The study was done in anticipation of the WTO working party members asking Ethiopia to open up its financial sector to some degree of foreign participation. The study states, “in order to respond to such a request in an appropriate way, it is indispensable for the government to have thorough information about the likely consequences of different types of liberalisation for both the financial sector and the economy in general.”
 

The study, begun in January 2007 and financed by the World Bank (WB), was a collaboration of seven experts, three of which are Ethiopian, Solomon Ababy, Gebrehiwot Ageba and Desalegn Yizengaw. With the aim to provide information concerning the outcomes of financial liberalisation, the study also makes recommendations as to the path that Ethiopia should take.
 

The potential benefits of financial services liberalisation, according to the study, are increased domestic competition, leading to a higher quality of services and access and the potential to serve as a catalyst for domestic reform by creating a constituency for improved regulation and disclosure. In addition, the inclusion of foreign banks in the local system would inject additional capital into the local economy.
 

Additional potential benefits might come in domestic savings mobilisation, nationwide availability of and access to banking services and the development of a viable domestic banking industry with the ability to compete with international banks.
 

But the liberalisation of the financial services sector in Ethiopia also faces a number of risks and uncertainties. These could include liberalisation not yielding a more stable source of credit for domestic borrowers, as, in the early stages of the process, foreign banks may capture the most desirable markets and customers, leaving the domestic banks with higher-risk assets and customers. Additionally, foreign banks may also leave the market during financial crises, creating a capital flight and leading to the potential for rapid currency depreciation.
 

The study states that the economy would have grown an additional 0.5pc faster during 1990-2004 if it had the same degree of financial openness as countries classified in the ‘open’ category by the WTO. But with a closed capital account that is regulated heavily by the National Bank of Ethiopia (NBE), foreign involvement in the financial services sector is difficult.
 

“Unless Ethiopia is obliged to liberalise its capital account, after WTO accession, foreign banks will be subjected to foreign exchange regulation pertaining to the capital account,” Eyob Tesfaye (PhD), member of the WTO technical committee, told Fortune.
 

The study suggests that, if foreign banks were permitted to enter into the Ethiopian economy, there would likely to be a narrowing of the efficiency gap and interest rate spread between the public and private banks and a consequent improvement in Ethiopia’s national economic welfare.
 

Ahmed Tussa, state minister of Trade and Industry, stated in his opening speech at the meeting, the government acknowledges that WTO membership will create confidence for investors and serve as one of the instruments to attract Foreign Direct Investment (FDI), and could also help secure predictable transparent market access for our exports.
 

“The study should have rejected the full participation of foreign banks. If foreign banks are allowed to come in, they will concentrate on international bank accounts, and, as a result of the competition, domestic banks will be forced to close their branches, threatening the financial deepening process of the country,” Eyob stated.

Ethiopia is one of the most unbanked countries, according to the study, needing more branches. Only two per cent of the population have savings accounts.
