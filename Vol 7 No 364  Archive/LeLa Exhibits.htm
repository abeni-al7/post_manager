LeLa Exhibits Quiet Elegance

  
 

 

  
     
 
 















 

  

Lela has many meanings if you take it in the context of different languages. In Amharic, it means different, unique, or something else. In French, it is two words, the male-le and female-la, the definite articles.

Or simply the name could be a play on the first letters of the owner of Lela Gallery, Lily Sahale, and guest curator Leo Lefort.

Opening its doors to the public with a splashy exhibition on Friday, April 13, 2007, Lela is indeed different and unique from any display spaces the Addis Abeba art scene has ever seen.

Comfortably situated on Jimma Road, opposite the route going to Alert Hospital, the space used for the exhibition was formerly a factory, lying on gracious grounds and offering seven metres in ceiling height.

Leo, in charge of assembling the all star artistic cast that participated in the exhibition said that such an event is a process that does not have to be defined by the space.

"This is an art factory, it is a place where artists can come to work, collectors to enjoy art, where you can not have yellow page exhibitions, but that features real and modern art," said Leo.

Lily, for whom the gallery is a sort of retirement plan, decided to invest in the gallery because she is passionate about art.

"I wanted to create a space for art lovers and for artists alike, so they would have a refuge that they could come to and view and display works," said Lily.

Featuring the works of 17 contemporary Ethiopian artists, the exhibition is entitled ‘Zero Gravity’. On the brochure handed out during the opening, Leo wrote "Some say that there is nothing more beautiful than a mathematical formula - I am not one of them - but if it is a matter of illustrating natural phenomena, art can often capture these more precisely than any learned treatise based on scientific research. While there are many artworks that refer to space, the aim of this exhibition is rather to present a fragment, and only that, of present-day Ethiopia."

All but two of the artists featured are graduates of the Addis Abeba University's (AAU) School of Fine Arts and Design, some even teach at their alma mater,  Carlos Marine, a Spanish artist living in Harar, was the only foreign artist featured during the exhibition.

With eye-catching pieces displayed in the large 20m by 12m space, the high ceilings and the florescent lighting make the exhibition more than rewarding to the viewer. Upon entering through the narrow door on the left corner of one of the larger walls, one is engulfed by a sense of light, openness and space. There is another door immediately to the left which leads to a quaint café  that offers a rotating display. The same high ceilings and white walls are present in this space, offering simple local chairs with a traditional twinge to them. The bar is done in wood and glass that has etchings. Although minimalist in appearance and choice of furniture, the café has a sense of cosiness and warmth that adds to the character of the gallery.

The paintings of Carlos Marine, done in Indian ink on paper are featured in this space. Displaying 10 pieces of semi-abstract impressions of Harar, its environment and people, the paintings are playful and would grab anyone's attention for their detail and distortions of form.

There is a display space for pottery and quilting that can be entered through the café from the outside, before entering into the main gallery. The walls are painted a deep pink which accent the natural clay, black, beige and silver material displayed in the room.

In the main gallery, beginning the display on the left wall with eight paintings is Dereje Demisse, a 2002 graduate of the School of Fine Arts. Using simple colours and broad strokes, this abstract artist displayed landscapes that had an almost child like innocent feel to them. One can not help being transported back to their youth when looking at Dereje's pieces, because of his style.

Following him was a huge piece by Tamrat Gezahegn, class of 2002, that featured a nude woman done with distortions to the hands, feet, and features. Taking up the length of the piece, the woman had three Amharic letters encased in circles over her that read TE BA KI, meaning keeper or guard in Amharic. The display on this wall is ended with the second entrance into the café.

Straight ahead are two canvases by Ermias Mazengia, class of 2000. The quiet abstract works by this artist are not only unique in their use of strokes but offer a colour pallet that is not found in the art of other contemporary Ethiopian artists. Without forms or figures, and sans titles, the pieces are an exercise in meditation and contemplation. Captivating without being gauche, colourful without being gaudy, and dominating in a subtle and muted manner, Ermias' works are enjoyable, beautiful, and able to transport the artists emotions through the canvas to the viewer.

Two small canvases by Tibebe Terffa, class of 1973, and one of the more experienced artists featured at the exhibition, were bright textured pieces that were almost pastries to the eyes.

Turning at a right angle, one is surprised by a photograph, courtesy of Michael Tsegay, class of 2002. Poignant and noteworthy, the photo features two boys jumping naked into a river that had almost the same complexion as their skin. The boys were caught in motion and you could almost feel the wind on their skin and the warmth of the sunlight on the water reflecting their shadows. The proximity, angle and light in the photo are masterful.

In a closet sized enclave, you come face to face, or rather leg to face with the first installation piece exhibited. A foil man, sans head and hands hangs upside down from the low ceiling of the enclave. The simple wish to comprehend gravitates you to the title, 'Leave me alone, I am making my head'. Merid Tafesse, class of 1998, also displayed three additional pieces, small abstract charcoals on paper, about the size of dinner napkins.

Arguably the best placed display follows, 12 pieces of identical size are hung in two rows of six, inches apart from each other. Nuru Abegaz, class of 1998, has been dubbed 'Nuru Nastro' because of his innovative use of electrical tape. His pieces use this media for a colourful, mazelike, and totally enamouring experience to the eye.

Following Nuru, is Behailu Bezabih, class of 1980, featuring a canvas done in ocres and muted tones featuring three male figures, the two in the foreground playing guitars. The muted piece is ideal for art lovers that like texture that does not take control of the canvas.

Getahun Assefa, class of 1987, follows with his own muted pieces done in greens and earth colours. With female figurines floating in air as if bungee jumping, the figures are appealing and feminine, highlighting the softness and grace of a woman with their postures and the colours used by the artist.

Antonio Florente, popular photographer, displayed four black and white pieces of large pelicans swimming over the surface of a river. The somewhat blurred and almost misty images of nature and its going-ons were simple and had a screen saver look to them.

Dawit Abebe, class of 2001, displayed three canvases that had figures painted on top of a collage done using newspapers. The first canvas is a self portrait of the artist dressed in what appears to be feminine garb wearing a license plate across his chest. The other two canvases featured three male figures, which appeared to be sharing a scale on one and playing a game of twister on the other. One can not help being transferred back to the 1980s with the style and media chosen by the artist.

Kedir Keri, class of 1989, follows with a canvas showing a male and female figure in mid air. Calling them 'Adam and Eve', Kedir uses a simple style and form in his art.

The second of Michael's photographs is featured next, with a breathtaking picture of a man standing in a doorway at the end of a pitch black corridor. The photograph is taken from the other end of the hallway in the darkness. The viewer can not help but feel a glimmer of hope and a sense of freedom when looking at the piece.

Bekelle Mekonnen, class of 1983, and one of the Art School instructors featured at this exhibition, displayed the second installation piece of the show.

With a black chain standing vertically and crowned by a sphere with holes in it, the piece though small requires time. The sphere appears like a ball with snatches taken out of it roughly but that has somehow managed to keep its air. Looking inside there is patchwork and silver duct tape furthering that feeling of having something ripped or torn out. There is quiet desperation with small and infrequent glimpses of hope, and yet a strong sense of erect price, often the fall of a man.

Bisrat Shibabaw, class of 1985, and another instructor at the Art School, pulls you in, whether you like it or not, with a round piece done in an extremely bright red and black. With fireflies, plants and expert shading, the piece is impossible to miss, although it has been hung higher than any other painting on that wall. It is almost a shame having to look away from the use of colour and intricate brush work.

Mezgebu Tessema, class of 1980, the third featured instructor, displayed a painting of a large rock, small at the base and wide at the top. That may sound simple and even mundane enough, but the single piece displayed by this experienced artist shows not only great diligence in its exacting and photo-like detail, but artistic mastery in its ability to transport and make the viewer physically feel all the elements of the vision that is before him.

Ermias returns here with two more, larger canvases. Bold and beautiful, featuring the colours that have become the signatures of this artist, the large smooth works show a skill and talent that is beyond the years of this 29 year old.

Last, but never least, the artist that has often been argued as the first true Ethiopian abstract artist, and trained by Ethiopian art legend, Gebrekristos Desta, the ever active and thought invoking Yohannes Gedamu presented a single canvas of pure geometric abstraction. Using matte colours in deep dense tones, the artist's use of spheres and cones were so well mastered that they transcended their usual forms. The bold colours looked almost powdery in their texture further adding to the depth of the piece.

Lela Gallery held this exhibition as the launch of its plan to develop an art space for artists and artisans in Ethiopia.  With the café, the display store and the exhibition space, Lily and Leo plan to develop the space into a haven for local and international artists to do residency programmes. The gallery will offer between six and eight exhibitions a year, each running at least a month.

"This is the kind of space and energy that the art scene in Ethiopia needs right now," said Ermias Mazengia. "This is the perfect display space for whatever size canvas and when the residency programmes get started, what better place could there be to paint?"

This exhibition ends on May 30, 2007.

 
By LULIT AMDEMARIAM
FORTUNE STAFF WRITER
